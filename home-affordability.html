Mortgage Affordability Guide

Home Affordability: How Much House Can You Honestly Afford?

One of the most common questions buyers ask is how much house can I afford. The answer is not based only on income or the size of your down payment. Lenders usually look at your full financial picture, including monthly debts, mortgage payment, credit profile, cash reserves, and the stability of your income.

A home affordability calculator can help estimate a reasonable price range before you start touring homes. This matters because the maximum amount a lender may approve is not always the amount you should comfortably spend. A realistic budget should leave room for repairs, utilities, furniture, maintenance, savings, insurance, property taxes, and unexpected life events.

The 28/36 rule for home buying is a popular guideline used to evaluate affordability. The first number means your monthly housing payment should ideally stay around 28% or less of your gross monthly income. The second number means your total monthly debt payments, including housing, student loans, credit cards, auto loans, and other debts, should generally stay around 36% or less of gross monthly income.

This is why the mortgage debt to income ratio is so important. A borrower with a high income but large monthly debt payments may qualify for less than expected. On the other hand, a buyer with moderate income and very little debt may have stronger borrowing capacity. DTI does not measure everything, but it gives lenders a quick view of how much of your monthly income is already committed.

Your down payment also affects your maximum home purchase price. A larger down payment may reduce the loan amount, lower monthly payments, improve lender confidence, and sometimes help avoid private mortgage insurance. However, using all available cash for the down payment can be risky if it leaves you without emergency savings or repair reserves after closing.

A smart home-buying budget starts with the monthly payment, not just the sale price. Buyers should estimate principal and interest, property taxes, homeowners insurance, HOA dues, utilities, maintenance, and possible mortgage insurance. A home that looks affordable based on the mortgage alone may feel expensive once real ownership costs are included.
