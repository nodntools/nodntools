Payroll & Labor Guide

Understanding Overtime Rules: How to Calculate Your Extra Pay

If you are wondering how to calculate overtime pay, the basic idea is simple: multiply your regular hourly wage by the overtime rate multiplier, then multiply that overtime rate by the number of overtime hours worked. For many hourly workers in the United States, overtime is commonly calculated after 40 hours in a workweek.

A time and a half calculator uses a 1.5× multiplier. For example, if your regular hourly wage is $20, your time-and-a-half overtime rate is $30 per hour. If you worked 5 overtime hours, your overtime pay would be $150. This amount is added to your regular pay to estimate your total gross pay before taxes and deductions.

The FLSA overtime rules are a major reference point in the United States. The Fair Labor Standards Act generally requires covered nonexempt employees to receive overtime pay at not less than one and one-half times their regular rate of pay for hours worked over 40 in a workweek. However, exemptions, salary basis rules, job duties, state laws, and industry-specific rules can affect eligibility.

Overtime pay for hourly workers can also vary in Canada and across U.S. states. Some jurisdictions may have daily overtime rules, special rules for holidays, or different standards for certain industries. Workers should always compare their paystub against the rules that apply to their province, state, employer, union agreement, or contract.

Double time wages are typically calculated at 2× the regular hourly rate. Double time may apply in specific circumstances depending on local law, employment contract, union rules, weekend schedules, holiday policies, or daily overtime thresholds. It is not automatic everywhere, so workers should confirm the exact rule that applies to their workplace.

Paystub checklist for overtime

  • Check total hours: Confirm regular and overtime hours are separated correctly.
  • Check the multiplier: Make sure overtime is paid at 1.5×, 2×, or the applicable rate.
  • Check unpaid breaks: Verify break deductions match your time records.
  • Check local rules: Some areas have daily overtime, holiday pay, or special industry rules.
