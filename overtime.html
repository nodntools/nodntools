How to Use the Overtime Calculator

Start by entering your regular hourly wage. This is your base pay rate before overtime premiums. Next, enter the number of regular hours worked. For many U.S. hourly workers, regular weekly hours are often counted up to 40 hours in a workweek, but this can vary depending on local rules and employer policies. Then enter the number of overtime hours worked. Finally, choose an overtime multiplier. The most common overtime multiplier is 1.5×, often called time and a half. Some situations may use 2×, often called double time.

After you enter the numbers, the calculator instantly displays total gross pay, hourly wage, overtime rate, regular hours, overtime hours, regular pay, overtime pay, total hours, and effective hourly rate. The effective hourly rate can be helpful because it shows your average pay across all hours after including overtime premiums. For example, if your base wage is $25 per hour, your time-and-a-half overtime rate is $37.50 per hour. If you work 40 regular hours and 5 overtime hours, the overtime premium increases your total gross pay and your effective average hourly rate.

How to Calculate Overtime Pay

The basic overtime formula is straightforward. First, multiply your hourly wage by the overtime multiplier to find the overtime rate. Then multiply that overtime rate by the number of overtime hours worked. Regular pay is calculated separately by multiplying the regular hourly wage by regular hours. Total gross pay equals regular pay plus overtime pay. This amount is gross pay, meaning it is calculated before taxes, payroll deductions, retirement contributions, health insurance premiums, wage garnishments, or other adjustments.

For example, suppose your regular hourly wage is $20 per hour and your overtime multiplier is 1.5×. Your overtime rate is $30 per hour. If you work 40 regular hours and 8 overtime hours, your regular pay is $800 and your overtime pay is $240. Your total gross pay would be $1,040 before deductions. If double time applies at 2×, the overtime rate would be $40 per hour, and 8 double-time hours would equal $320 in overtime pay.

Time and a Half Explained

Time and a half means an employee is paid one and one-half times the regular hourly rate for qualifying overtime hours. The multiplier is 1.5. If the regular rate is $18 per hour, time and a half is $27 per hour. If the regular rate is $25 per hour, time and a half is $37.50 per hour. This type of overtime premium is common for covered nonexempt hourly workers in many U.S. workweek overtime situations, but actual eligibility depends on employment classification and applicable law.

A time and a half calculator is helpful because overtime pay is not simply extra hours at the regular rate. The premium rate can increase the value of each additional hour significantly. Workers can use this estimate to decide whether extra shifts are worth the time, compare schedules, budget for upcoming expenses, or check whether a paycheck roughly matches expected hours.

Double Time Explained

Double time means the employee is paid twice the regular hourly wage for qualifying hours. If a worker earns $20 per hour, double time is $40 per hour. Double time may apply under certain state or provincial laws, union contracts, employer policies, holiday pay rules, emergency schedules, or long-shift thresholds. It is not automatic in every workplace, so workers should confirm when double time applies before relying on the estimate.

Employers and workers should carefully distinguish between overtime, holiday pay, premium pay, shift differentials, and bonuses. Some payments may be calculated separately or included differently depending on the payroll system and applicable rules. This calculator provides a simple gross pay estimate based on the selected multiplier, but payroll departments may use more detailed calculations.

Important Payroll Details This Calculator Does Not Include

This calculator estimates gross pay only. It does not calculate final take-home pay. Your paycheck may be reduced by income tax withholding, Social Security, Medicare, Canada Pension Plan, Employment Insurance, state or provincial taxes, health insurance premiums, retirement contributions, union dues, wage garnishments, or other deductions. It also does not include tips, bonuses, commissions, paid time off, holiday premiums, shift differentials, retroactive pay, expense reimbursements, or employer-specific adjustments.

Overtime eligibility can also depend on more than hours worked. Some salaried employees may be exempt from overtime depending on job duties and legal classification. Some employees may have special rules under union agreements, public sector policies, healthcare schedules, transportation rules, agricultural rules, construction rules, or local labor laws. Because rules vary, this calculator should be used as a planning estimate rather than a final legal answer.

Paystub Checklist for Overtime

When reviewing overtime pay, compare your timesheet with your paystub. Check that regular hours and overtime hours are separated correctly. Confirm that the overtime multiplier matches your workplace rule. Review unpaid meal breaks, paid breaks, shift premiums, holiday rules, and daily overtime rules if they apply. Also check whether your employer uses a weekly, biweekly, semi-monthly, or monthly pay period, because pay periods can affect how hours appear on a paycheck.

If your pay appears incorrect, gather your time records, schedule, paystub, employment agreement, and workplace policy. Then contact your manager, HR department, payroll provider, union representative, or local labor authority. Clear records make payroll questions easier to resolve. This calculator can help you estimate the expected gross pay, but official payroll records and applicable laws determine the final answer.

Why Effective Hourly Rate Matters

The effective hourly rate shows your average hourly earnings after combining regular pay and overtime pay. This number can help compare different schedules. For example, a 45-hour week with 5 overtime hours may produce a higher effective hourly rate than a 40-hour week at the same base wage. However, effective pay is only one factor. Extra hours can also affect rest, childcare, commuting, health, family time, and work-life balance.

Workers can use the effective hourly rate to make informed decisions about overtime opportunities. Employers can use it to understand labor cost. In both cases, the calculation supports better planning. A transparent pay estimate can reduce confusion and help everyone understand the financial impact of additional hours.

Frequently Asked Questions

Overtime Calculator FAQ

How do I calculate overtime pay?

Multiply your regular hourly wage by the overtime multiplier, then multiply that overtime rate by your overtime hours.

What is time and a half?

Time and a half means 1.5 times your regular hourly wage. A $20 hourly wage becomes $30 per overtime hour.

What is double time?

Double time means 2 times your regular hourly wage. A $20 hourly wage becomes $40 per double-time hour.

Does this calculator show take-home pay?

No. It estimates gross pay before taxes, deductions, benefits, tips, bonuses, and payroll adjustments.

Are overtime rules the same everywhere?

No. Overtime rules vary by country, state, province, industry, job classification, union agreement, and employer policy.

Can salaried employees get overtime?

Some salaried employees may qualify for overtime depending on local law, pay level, job duties, and exemption status.
