How to Calculate a Discount Percentage

The basic discount formula is simple: multiply the original price by the discount percentage, then subtract the discount amount from the original price. If an item costs $100 and the discount is 20%, the savings are $20. The final sale price before tax is $80. Written as a formula, the sale price equals original price multiplied by one minus the discount rate.

For example, a $250 jacket with a 30% discount saves $75 because $250 multiplied by 0.30 equals $75. The discounted price before tax is $175. If sales tax is 8%, the estimated tax is $14, and the final price is $189. This discount calculator performs the same process automatically and updates the result as you change the numbers.

A percentage discount is not always the only factor. Some retailers also offer fixed-dollar coupons, loyalty rewards, store credit, free shipping thresholds, or category-specific promotions. This version focuses on percentage discounts and optional tax because those are among the most common sale price calculations.

Double Discount Calculator: Why 30% Off Plus 20% Off Is Not 50% Off

Double discounts are one of the most misunderstood parts of retail math. When a store advertises an item as 30% off plus an extra 20% off, the discounts are usually applied one after the other. The second discount is applied to the already discounted price, not to the original price. That means the effective discount is lower than simply adding the two percentages together.

For example, start with a $100 product. A 30% discount lowers the price to $70. An additional 20% coupon then applies to $70, reducing the price by $14. The final price before tax is $56. The total savings are $44, which means the effective discount is 44%, not 50%. This is why a double discount calculator is helpful when comparing stacked coupons, extra sale codes, loyalty discounts, and clearance promotions.

Retailers may apply discounts in different orders depending on coupon rules. Some coupons may exclude sale items, some may apply only before tax, some may require a minimum purchase, and some may work only for certain product categories. Always review the final checkout page before deciding whether a discount is truly worth it.

Smart Shopping Tips for Better Deal Comparison

Always compare the final price, not only the percentage off. A product with a 60% discount may still be expensive if the original price was inflated. Another store with a smaller discount may offer a better final price, better warranty, easier returns, or lower shipping cost. A sale price calculator helps you separate emotional marketing from practical savings.

Check whether tax, shipping, and handling are included. Many shoppers calculate the discount correctly but forget taxes or delivery fees. If one store offers free shipping and another charges a shipping fee, the final checkout total may change the better deal. This is especially important for furniture, electronics, large appliances, international orders, and marketplace sellers.

Watch for coupon restrictions. Some coupons exclude clearance items, gift cards, electronics, luxury brands, third-party sellers, subscription products, or already-discounted merchandise. Some coupons also expire quickly or require a minimum order value. Review the terms before relying on a coupon percentage.

Use a budget before shopping during big sale events. Black Friday, Cyber Monday, Boxing Day, Prime Day, back-to-school sales, and holiday promotions can create urgency. A calculator helps you verify the math, but a budget helps you decide whether the purchase makes sense in the first place.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate a discount?

Multiply the original price by the discount percentage, then subtract that amount from the original price.

How does a double discount work?

A double discount is usually applied in sequence. The first discount reduces the original price, then the second discount applies to the reduced price.

Is 30% off plus 20% off the same as 50% off?

No. If discounts are applied one after another, 30% off plus 20% off equals a 44% effective discount.

Should tax be calculated before or after discount?

Many retailers apply tax after the discount, but rules vary by location, product type, and retailer policy.

Can this calculator include shipping fees?

This version focuses on price, percentage discounts, coupons, and tax. Add shipping separately when comparing final checkout totals.
