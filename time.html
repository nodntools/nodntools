How to Calculate Hours Worked

To calculate hours worked manually, subtract the start time from the end time. If the shift starts at 9:00 AM and ends at 5:30 PM, the elapsed time is 8 hours and 30 minutes. If there is a 30-minute unpaid break, the paid work time is 8 hours. If a shift starts at 10:00 PM and ends at 6:00 AM the next day, the elapsed time is 8 hours because the end time crosses midnight. A calculator is helpful because it handles both same-day and overnight logic automatically.

Break deductions are important because paid time and elapsed time are not always the same. A person may be present for 8.5 hours but paid for 8 hours after a 30-minute unpaid meal break. A freelancer may work for 3.5 hours without a break and bill the full 3.5 hours. A manager may need to check whether a timesheet includes required unpaid breaks. A time calculator creates a clear and consistent record.

Decimal Hours Explained

Decimal hours convert minutes into fractions of an hour. This format is common in payroll systems, invoices, timesheets, project reports, and time-tracking software. Instead of writing 7 hours and 30 minutes, decimal hours show 7.50 hours. Instead of 6 hours and 15 minutes, decimal hours show 6.25 hours. Instead of 4 hours and 45 minutes, decimal hours show 4.75 hours. Decimal format makes it easier to multiply time by an hourly rate.

For example, if a freelancer works 3 hours and 30 minutes at $60 per hour, the decimal time is 3.50 hours. The invoice amount is 3.50 × $60, or $210. If an employee works 7 hours and 45 minutes, the decimal time is 7.75 hours. Payroll systems often use decimal time because it reduces ambiguity and supports automated calculations.

Break Deductions and Paid Time

Breaks are one of the most common sources of time calculation errors. A work shift may include a meal break, rest break, coffee break, personal break, or unpaid interruption. Some breaks are paid, while others are unpaid. This depends on employer policy, contract terms, jurisdiction, and labor rules. This calculator does not decide whether a break should be paid or unpaid. It simply deducts the break minutes you enter from the elapsed time so you can calculate a clean paid-time estimate.

When using the tool for a time card, enter only break minutes that should be deducted. If a break is paid, do not enter it as a deduction. If a lunch break is unpaid, enter the lunch minutes. If your company uses automatic break deductions, compare the calculator result with official payroll rules. For compliance-sensitive payroll, always confirm with an employer, HR department, accountant, payroll provider, or qualified labor professional.

Overnight Shift Time Calculation

Overnight shifts can be confusing because the end time may appear earlier than the start time. For example, a shift that starts at 10:00 PM and ends at 6:00 AM crosses midnight. A simple subtraction would look wrong if it treated both times as the same day. This calculator handles that case by adding 24 hours to the end time when the end time is earlier than the start time. The result is treated as a shift ending the next day.

Overnight shift support is useful for healthcare workers, security staff, drivers, warehouse teams, hotel employees, restaurant staff, manufacturing workers, support agents, emergency workers, event staff, freelancers, and anyone who works across midnight. If a shift spans more than 24 hours, a more detailed date-based time calculator may be needed, but for common overnight shifts this tool provides a quick estimate.
