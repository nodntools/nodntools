Gig Worker Tax Guide

Side Hustle Taxes: How Much Should You Legally Set Aside?

Side hustle income can feel exciting until tax season arrives. If you have a main W-2 job and also earn freelance, creator, delivery, consulting, tutoring, design, resale, or gig income, your side income is usually added on top of your regular job income. That means the tax bracket for side income may be higher than many new freelancers expect, because the extra income is not taxed in isolation.

A common question is how much to set aside for side hustle taxes. A practical starting point for many U.S. gig workers is to reserve roughly 25% to 35% of net side hustle profit, depending on income level, state taxes, deductions, and whether quarterly estimated payments are required. Higher earners or people in high-tax states may need to set aside more, while very small side businesses may need less.

The reason side hustle taxes can feel expensive is that 1099 income often does not have payroll withholding. Employees typically see federal tax, Social Security, and Medicare withheld automatically. By contrast, independent contractors may need a self-employment tax estimated calculator because they are generally responsible for both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare. In the United States, the simplified self-employment tax rate is often estimated at 15.3% before other income taxes.

Legal deductions can make a major difference. Many 1099 write offs for side hustles reduce taxable business profit when they are ordinary and necessary for the work. Depending on the activity, examples may include a laptop, software subscriptions, advertising, website hosting, bookkeeping tools, business mileage, home office expenses, payment processing fees, shipping supplies, professional services, and a business portion of phone or internet costs.

Side Hustle Example Possible Write-Offs Tax Planning Tip
Freelance writing Laptop, research tools, website, software Track invoices and platform fees monthly
Delivery or rideshare Mileage, phone, tolls, supplies Keep a detailed mileage log
Online selling Inventory, shipping, marketplace fees Separate cost of goods from profit

For example, taxes on freelance writing side hustle income may depend on more than the amount clients paid you. A writer might deduct writing software, a portfolio website, business-use internet, payment processing fees, courses, and professional services when those expenses are properly documented and directly related to the work. Good records are often the difference between guessing at tax time and confidently reporting income.

A smart system is to create a separate tax savings account. Each time you receive side hustle income, transfer a percentage of net profit into that account before spending the rest. This habit helps avoid a surprise tax bill and makes quarterly estimated payments easier. Many freelancers also review income and expenses monthly so they can adjust their set-aside percentage before the year ends.
